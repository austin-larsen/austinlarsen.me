Google just filed civil litigation against the phishing-as-a-service provider known as Darcula to disrupt their large-scale fraud operations. Our team at Google Threat Intelligence Group has been tracking this group, which was responsible for 80 percent of all phishing texts during a peak period earlier this year.

That number deserves a moment. At peak, the majority of phishing texts your users were receiving were coming from a single criminal platform.

## The Magic Cat Platform

Darcula's Magic Cat software is designed to eliminate the technical barrier to entry for fraud. It allows scammers with limited technical skill to impersonate trusted organizations at scale: the US Postal Service, the IRS, E-ZPass, and hundreds of other brands that people expect to receive texts from.

The result of this activity was the theft of nearly 900,000 credit card numbers globally, including 40,000 from victims in the United States. These are not abstract statistics. Each number represents a real person who received a convincing text message, clicked through to a convincing fake page, and handed over their financial information.

## Why Civil Litigation

The lawsuit is designed to give Google legal standing to seize websites the group relies on for their operations. This is not the same as a criminal referral. It is a direct enforcement mechanism that can move faster and does not require establishing criminal jurisdiction in another country.

Criminal infrastructure disruption through technical means is a continuous process. Hosting changes. Domains rotate. Litigation creates a different category of sustained pressure on the actors and gives us tools to act on infrastructure that technical remediation alone cannot reach.

## The Broader Strategy

This filing is part of a broader commitment to use every technical and legal tool available to protect users and hold cybercriminal actors accountable. We have seen this approach work against other platforms. The goal is not a single decisive blow against a criminal operation. It is to make the operation increasingly expensive and risky to run.
